*Why yoyopod exists and who is building it.*

## The gap before the smartphone

Kids ages 7–14 want independence before parents are ready to hand over
a smartphone. yoyopod is built for exactly that gap — a parent-managed
**first independent device** that gives kids focused independence
(communication, music, mobility) and gives parents peace of mind
through reliable calling and live-ish location, without a smartphone's
distraction, complexity, and screen addiction.

The positioning line, verbatim:

> **Safer independence for kids. Peace of mind for parents. Before the
> smartphone.**

"The first device before a smartphone" names a category, not a
competitor. The public story is built around three parent questions, in
this order: *Can I reach my child? · Can they reach me? · Can I see
where they are?* yoyopod is built around those questions first.

## Our answer

| Attribute | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Buyer | parents |
| User | kids ages 7–14 |
| Product type | parent-managed connected companion device |
| Form factor | walkie-talkie-like, tiny screen, minimal input |

Four V1 pillars carry the promise:

1. **Whitelist calls and voice messages** — approved contacts only.
2. **Live-ish location** — visibility when it matters. (The hedge is
   deliberate: it is not marketed as real-time tracking.)
3. **Music and audio** — an everyday audio companion.
4. **A parent mobile app** — contacts, settings, and device controls
   stay with the parent.

The emotional promise splits cleanly: for the kid, independence — a
device that feels grown-up and is genuinely theirs; for the parent,
safety and peace of mind. Two trust anchors carry it: **reliable
reachability** and **trustworthy location**. Everything else is
secondary — including everything the answer refuses to be
([What yoyopod Is Not](/company/what-we-are-not/)).

> For kids, YoYoPod feels like freedom.
> For parents, it feels like reassurance.
> For both, it's a better step before the smartphone years.

## Who we are

Software-engineer fathers in Stuttgart, Germany, building the device we
wanted for our own kids — which means the product gets tested on its
actual users at our own dinner tables. The engineering choices trace
straight back to the mission: one button and a small calm screen
because simplicity *is* the product, whitelisted calling and voice
notes because safe communication is pillar one, cellular + GPS because
reachability and location are the trust anchors.

The work happens in the open at
[github.com/attmous/yoyopod](https://github.com/attmous/yoyopod); the
as-built engineering docs live in the main docs site (`website/` in the
repository). The rules that keep us honest are written down as
[Product Principles](/company/principles/).

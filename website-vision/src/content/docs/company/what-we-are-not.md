## What we are not

The anti-positioning list, verbatim from the positioning doc:

> Not a smartphone replacement · not a toy · not a screen-first device ·
> not another addictive kids gadget. *"It's a simpler first step."*

*Proposed — the ideal design, not yet adopted.*

**Not a smartphone replacement.** The temptation arrives politely, one
parent request at a time: just a browser, just a messenger, just one
exception. Every one of them is a small step toward the very thing
families bought yoyopod to postpone. We refuse because the product's
whole promise is sequencing — yoyopod comes *before* the smartphone,
buys childhood a few more years, and hands over gracefully when the
time comes. The moment it tries to replace a smartphone, it competes
with one, on a tiny canvas, having given up its reason to exist.

**Not a toy.** The temptation is the toy aisle: bright molds, licensed
characters, a price that says birthday present. Toys are wonderful,
and toys get outgrown — that is exactly the problem. A yoyopod is
bought by a parent for daily use across years: real calls to real
family, real music, a real place in the school bag. Refusing the toy
label is not dismissing play — play is half of what the device is for.
It is refusing the shelf where things go once the novelty wears off.

**Not a screen-first device.** The temptation is economics: a bigger
canvas would demo better, and every future feature becomes "just
another screen." We refuse because screen-light *is* the product, not
a limitation of it. The canvas shows a name, a code, what's playing —
then rests. The moment it becomes a destination instead of a glance,
yoyopod is a small, bad tablet, and the parents who chose it precisely
to avoid one would be right to leave.

**Not another addictive kids gadget.** The temptation is every growth
mechanic that demonstrably works: streaks, badges, collectibles, "your
friend just…". They all move the same metric — time on device — and
time on device is the one number we want low. A yoyopod moment ends on
purpose: the call finishes, the story finishes, the kid goes back out
the door. It's a simpler first step, and simpler means we don't get to
borrow the tricks.

## What we will not market

The do-not-say list, verbatim:

> Do **not** market yoyopod as: *AI for kids* · *a new communication
> platform* · *a smart wearable* · *an educational device*. Each
> dilutes the sharpest truth: **it is the first device before a
> smartphone.**

*Proposed — the ideal design, not yet adopted.*

**"AI for kids."** The label that raises money fastest and trust
slowest. yoyopod uses AI narrowly and always disclosed: a spoken
answer when a child holds the button and [asks](/apps/ask/). Marketing
the AI as the product inverts that — suddenly the model is the hero,
every model update is a product launch, and every AI headline, good or
bad, becomes our headline. It sells for a season and then defines us
by the one ingredient we deliberately kept small.

**"A new communication platform."** Platform language promises growth
in exactly the directions we refuse: more contacts, more channels,
network effects. yoyopod's calling is deliberately small — a whitelist
a parent curates, family on the other end, nothing viral about it.
Claiming the platform sells a roadmap we will never ship, and invites
every comparison we intend to lose.

**"A smart wearable."** The wearable frame drags in expectations we
won't meet — sensors, health metrics, notifications on the wrist — and
a data posture we won't accept, because wearables normalize continuous
measurement of the person wearing them. yoyopod measures almost
nothing, on purpose: [location](/families/location/) is live-ish and
coarse, and a child profile is a first name and an age band. "Smart
wearable" quietly promises the opposite, and we would spend years
walking it back.

**"An educational device."** The easiest sale in the kids' aisle —
everything sells better with learning on the box — and the slowest
corrosion, because the label converts curiosity into curriculum. An
educational device needs outcomes, progress bars, assessment: homework
in the product. Help Agents on the Ask wheel answer a question because
a kid was curious, not because a lesson plan said so. The honest claim
is smaller and stronger: the first device before a smartphone.

## Why we write this down

The refusals are already treated as strategy in the repository: the
positioning doc is the messaging source of truth, with the decisions
made — when writing anything public about yoyopod, start there — and
both lists above are part of those decisions, not aspirations. The
category claimed instead is the hero headline itself: **"The first
device before a smartphone."** Every ruled-out label is ruled out for
the same documented reason: each dilutes that sharpest truth.

This page is the fuller version of the refusal list cross-referenced
from [Product Principles](/company/principles/), and the fence around
what may ever appear on the [Roadmap](/company/roadmap/).

## Open questions

- **Adopt the short argued paragraphs** on this page as the public tone
  of the refusal list — or publish dry one-liners and keep the
  arguments internal.
- **Adopt the "play is half the point" framing** of the not-a-toy
  refusal — or rework the phrasing until it can never be read as
  dismissive of play.
- **Decide whether the list is closed:** whether it may grow at all,
  and whether changing it requires the same sign-off as changing the
  hero headline it exists to protect.

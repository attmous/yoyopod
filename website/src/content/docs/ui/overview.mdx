yoyopod's entire user interface lives on one small pane of glass: a 240×280
RGB565 panel driven over SPI, controlled with a single physical button. This
page is the guided tour — every layer involved in presenting any shape or
animation on the glass, at 30,000 feet. Each stop links to the deep page for
that layer. (If you want one picture to hang all of this on first, read
[The Mental Model](/ui/mental-model/).)

## The system in five rules

The design handoff package distills the whole UI into five rules. Everything
else in this guide is machinery in service of them.

<CardGrid>
	<Card title="Three gestures, one button" icon="right-caret">
		Press = advance/roll · double-press = open/activate · long-press = go
		Home. The prototype has exactly one physical button; "hold to talk" is
		that same button remapped per screen.
	</Card>

## The stack at 30,000 feet

<Layers class="diagram" />

Two layers are ours (screens/scene graph and the engine), one is stock
open-source (LVGL 9.5.0, used strictly as a renderer), and two are
hardware-facing (the userspace Whisplay driver and the panel itself). The
UI runs as its own binary, `yoyopod-ui-host`, supervised by
`yoyopod-runtime` over a line protocol — the
[Advanced section](/ui/advanced/architecture/) dissects that process
model, and the [Runtime & Workers Guide](/runtime/) documents the
supervisor's side in full.

## The pixel tour

What happens between "a screen wants to show something" and light leaving
the glass, in ten steps:

1. **A screen component builds a scene graph** — a declarative tree of
   containers, labels, images, and decks in `device/ui/src/components/`.
   → [Screens & Navigation](/ui/screens/)
2. **The engine diffs the new scene against the old one** and emits only
   the mutations that changed, under a hard budget of 60 live widgets.
   → [The Custom Framework](/ui/framework/)
3. **Animations advance on the same tick** — a custom timeline engine
   (keyframes, easing, presets), not LVGL's animation system.
   → [Motion & Theming](/ui/motion-and-theming/)
4. **The facade applies mutations to LVGL objects** through a hand-written
   C FFI — there is no Rust binding crate. → [The LVGL Layer](/ui/lvgl/)
5. **LVGL renders the dirty areas** in partial mode into a single 240×40
   draw buffer. → [The Render Loop](/ui/rendering/)
6. **The flush callback byte-swaps every pixel** from little-endian RGB565
   (what LVGL emits) to big-endian (what the panel wants).
   → [The Render Loop](/ui/rendering/)
7. **Pixels land in a shadow framebuffer** — an in-memory `Vec<u16>`, not
   `/dev/fb0`; there is no kernel display driver at all.
   → [The Render Loop](/ui/rendering/)
8. **The worker decides what to push**: the full frame, just the status
   bar, or just the dirty region — and only if something is dirty.
   → [The Render Loop](/ui/rendering/)
9. **The driver streams bytes over SPI** at 100 MHz in 4096-byte chunks
   after setting the panel's address window.
   → [Talking to the Panel](/ui/driver/)
10. **The ST7789 lights the glass**: a 240×280 window into a 240×320
    controller, offset by 20 rows. → [The Glass and the Board](/ui/hardware/)
